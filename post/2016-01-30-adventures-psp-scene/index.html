Adventures in the PSP Scene

Jan 30, 2016 22 min read

In this post I’m going to explain my experiences in the PSP scene. The PSP scene opened many doors for me: meet new people, learn to use design programs, start programming for first time in my life, … Definitely, what I lived from 2007 to 2010 thanks to the PSP community was incredibly helpful for me from then on.

I’ve divided in several chapters the whole content. You can access these chapters from the table of contents below.

Chapter 1: The arrival

After asking several times Santa Claus to gift me a PSP for Christmas, I finally got it. It was 2006. If I remember well, at that time the PSP was one of the most powerful portable consoles together with the NintendoDS (actually, I don’t think there were many more apart from these two “monsters”). The console came in a pack which included the official game of the MotoGP championship (I was and I am a huge fan of motorcycle racing).

For some days I played MotoGP. By that time, I did not have a memory stick that could allowed me to save the game status, so the battery had to be charged and I had to be careful so as not to shutdown the PSP (otherwise, I would loose all my progress). Then, I was bought Grand Theft Auto: Vice City Stories (a really funny game when played with friends via wireless).

Chapter 2: The revelation

It was at mid-January or at the end of January 2007, when I saw the light: all the things that could be done with the console. I do not exactly remember what happened to my computer but it was not working well; thus, we called the technician to see if he could repair it. He came home and after repairing whatever happened to it, he saw my PSP on the table. I remember he asked me something like “Have you hacked it?”; obviously, at that time I knew enough about the computer argot to know he was referring to the execution of videogames (i.e. play a videogame without having acquired it). After answering “No” to his question, he opened his bag and took his PSP; then, he turned it on and… Crash Bandicoot was on the screen! Crash Bandicoot! I spent many hours playing that game during my childhood in the original PlayStation. How did he manage to get it for PSP? The answer: he “freed” the PSP via software. I knew that consoles were hacked via hardware by changing microchips, but at that time I would have never thought that it could be done by software with a permanent effect.

This revelation really motivated me to achieve the same result. I asked him how did he do it, and he told me “After investigating quite a lot…”. He also told me not to update after observing that my PSP firmware version was 2.81 (he had something strange as version… 3.03 OE). So I decided to “investigate”. That same evening I started to search in Google for ways to free the console. Surprisingly, there were many results… so many that I didn’t know how to start! In some sources the 3.03 version appeared, in others 2.71 SE, and in others 3.10. 2.71 SE was too low, and 3.10 was the newest, so I thought I would install 3.10. I have to recognize that I also felt more intelligent than the technician because I found something newer! Nevertheless, I was mistaken: I had installed an official firmware (of course, I could not run Crash Bandicoot). Precisely, I did what I was told not to do: update the firmware.

After a week, I decided to go to the shop where the technician worked to know if there was something I could do to hack the PSP. However, he just reminded me that he told me not to update… However, he again said me not to update any more until something compatible with my version appeared. Some months later (I think that by May or June, or even before), an exploit that allowed to hack the PSP appeared. It worked for the version 3.50 and required the game Lumines to be used. I was partially lucky: I had version 3.10 but not the game. Thus, the hunt for this game started! But I was not the only one: maybe hundreds of people were in the same situation and the game was discontinued. Thus, I had to fight for one unit in eBay (finally got one for about 30 €, which was not bad at all)!

Chapter 3: The day arrived…

In the end, I could finally hack my PSP! I prepared everything very well since I didn’t want anything to go wrong. By using the exploit of the Lumines game, I got my PSP downgraded to 1.50. Version 1.50 allowed to run homebrew without any kind of additional software (it was the key of all the PSP scene). From that version I could run an updating executable (in PSP all executables were called EBOOT.PBP) that installed a Custom Firmware (CF) in the console.

Another “challenging” decision had to be made. At that time there were two custom firmware families: the CFs made by Dark Alex and the CFs made by the M33 team.

  • Dark Alex was a Spanish coder, and probably was the most famous coder of the PSP scene; he released the Special Edition (SE) and Open Edition (OE) firmwares like 2.71 SE, 3.02 OE, 3.03 OE, 3.10 OE, 3.30 OE and 3.40 OE.

  • On the other hand, the M33 team was believed to be Russian, and had just released (at that time) the 3.51 M33 and 3.52 M33 firmwares. Dark Alex retired after releasing the 3.40 OE, so the work of the M33 team was the only active one (some months later, it was revealed that Dark Alex was actually part of the M33 team, so he had not retired).

After thinking a bit about what to do, I decided to install 3.52 M33 in my PSP, and 3.40 OE in my brother’s PSP. Fortunately, everything went quite well.

From that moment, I started to take advantage that a Custom Firmware had: play PlayStation games, other PSP games, and even I could make the CPU work faster so that games loaded faster! I also started getting into forums, concretely in a Spanish one called ESPALPSP (I believe it closed because of legal issues) which was one of the most crowded forums in Spain.

Chapter 4: Autoinstallers and bans

The forums taught me a lot about what could be done regarding design and PSP. One of the first things that caught my attention was that anybody could make autoinstallers for homebrew and custom firmwares for the console. Afterwards, these installers could be easily shared my uploading them to MegaUpload, RapidShare or MediaFire. Actually, I used one for installing version 3.52 M33 in my PSP (at that time, it was the best option if you did not know how to organize the folders for an installation).

I was so excited that as soon as a new update was released, I made an autoinstaller for it. Sometimes, I shared them with the PSP community to make the life of the other newbies easier.

Nevertheless, I have to say that most of the shared installers (not only mine), tended to crash while copying the files to the PSP. Therefore, almost everybody would finally do the installations manually when they understood what had to be done.

In ESPALPSP forums I met very friendly people. Some nicknames I remember now are Gladiator, The_Ugly, Sarek, Captiva, Amcymonse, Ozzblf or Hirakun. However, one day I was “forced” to leave. I put it in quotation marks because it was completely my fault since I sent some private messages promoting another website called EstrenosPSP.

EstenosPSP was quite recent at the time I spammed. In fact, I was spammed by another ESPALPSP user who turned out to be one the creators of the site (The_Ugly). The main reason I think I did that was because I had a strong longing for power: I wanted to become moderator of a forum to organize it the best I could. In a well-established website like ESPALPSP it was nearly impossible, so I thought: “Why don’t I try in a new one? If they see I start to organize and create content and bring new people, then I can become a moderator”. Thus, I sent some spam messages in ESPALPSP to attract new people, copied my main contributions, and organize design challenges.

ESPALPSP caught me quite quickly and I was banned. Since that moment, I would not return to that forum except for the design competitions (another account was created with the name BlackDemon, I believe).

Although I did my best to make EstrenosPSP successful (with a questionable methodology), the website did not take off. Even the participation of the administrators was extremely low, so the website was doomed to fail.

Chapter 5: Translations of plugins

I looked for a new forum where to participate and I found one called TeknoPSP. This site was much more focused on the PSP scene rather than ESPALPSP and EstrenosPSP, which put special emphasis on publishing games. This last activity never attracted my attention, so TeknoPSP fitted my interests perfectly.

TeknoPSP users mostly published content about programming, modifying existing plugins or customizing the hardware and software of the console. One of the things that I saw that I could do was to translate plugins from English into Spanish. Actually, it was a colleague from ESPALPSP, Gladiator, who suggested me to do it with him.

The plugins were the Recovery LE 3.80 and the Recovery LE 3.90 respectively. They were originally programmed by the Team Wildcard. They allowed to open the recovery menu at any moment in the PSP and to modify its values. It was a quite interesting software because the typical recovery menu could only be opened when powering on the PSP while pressing the R trigger.

The translation was done by editing the hexadecimal code of the program. The literals could be easily found in any HEX editor. Thus, we only had to translate these pieces of text, save the file and share it with th community.

Our work was not 100% accurate, but it was useful to some people (at least, they express gratitude for our contribution). The main reason was that the translated words could not be longer than the original English words; so in some cases, we just left the English words if the translation could not be understood with few characters.

I have looked for some images and I have found one of the translation of the VSH menu originally developed by Red Squirrel. You can see it below.

I have got this image from a Wordpress blog I created to post my projects. It stayed updated only for a week during June 2008. You can click here to access the blog (I hope it will never disappear).

Chapter 6: Learning to program the PSP

After creating autoinstallers and translating software, I asked myself: “How can these programs be done? Can I do them as well?”.

To answer these questions, I firstly did a quick search on Google and the main result was: “Kit de Programación PSP para Torpes” (in English, it would be something like “PSP Programming Kit for Dummies”). I couldn’t feel dumber at that time, so it was ideal for me to get deeper in the PSP programming world. By the way, the author of the installer (because it was barely more than an installer) was Marce82, who got certain prestige in the scene.

I followed the steps to create the classic “Hello World!” program. Here you have a sample of how it looked like. Obviously, I didn’t understand almost anything. I was a 14 years old teen without programming background (I got my first computer with 10 years and got access to Internet with 13). Anybody in my family or in my group of friends could help me to understand what was going on. To sum up, things were difficult for me.

As you may have seen in the previous link, the programming language was C. Consequently, I can tell that C was the first programming language I learnt. Nonetheless, I must admit that during this period of my programming life, what I usually did was to reuse code from other people to create my applications. I never dominated the language and understood its capabilities fully.

People from the PSP programming world recommended to learn C for computer before starting with PSP. I didn’t do it, but I tried: there was an “academy” in Barcelona which pretended to teach the language you chose. I wanted to learn C, of course, but can you guess what they taught me? Visual Basic. A wasted summer because I didn’t only learn the language I wanted, but also because the quality of the course was extremely poor so I learnt almost nothing.

From what I remember, the LUA programming language was also quite used. I believe that applications written in LUA could be run from another program that called LUA Player. The LUA language was heavily promoted in a community called SceneBeta (originally, betapesepe). In fact, depending on the forum you belonged to, there was a preference for C or LUA. In my case, the preference was for C, so from the beginning I didn’t see anything but C. On the other hand, there were some LUA programmers that started coding in C (e.g. a very talented guy called carlosgs is an example).

One kind of application that was very famous consisted in “hiding” some of the folders of the PSP. From the main menu of the console you could access your music, images or videos from the folders MUSIC, PHOTO or VIDEO respectively. Thus, if you renamed these folders to other names different to the official ones (e.g. XMUSIC, XPHOTO, XVIDEO), the PSP would not show any music, pictures or videos. So what most of these hiding apps did was to rename these folders.

I realised that I also could do this kind of application as soon as I understood how they worked. A proof of this are my two first applications: Ocultador 1.0 and Ocultador 2.0 (the word “ocultador” means “file hide”).

Below there’s an old screenshot I did when I published the second version in my forgotten blog in 2008. A console button was used for either hiding or showing an specific folder (three folders could be treated). Another button allowed the user to exit the application. I think that there wasn’t any message that told whether the folders were being modified, you had to look at the orange flashing LED of the PSP (which indicated that the memory card was accessed).

Obviously, new software which does the same as existing software without contributing anything original, is doomed to be ignored. And that’s what happened. However, as you will see afterwards, I kept working on it…

Chapter 7: Joining programming teams and new websites

Although I was quite proud of my barely original homebrews, I thought that if I joined someone to program with, I would improve much more. One day, I saw in the forums of TeknoPSP post that said something like: “programmer looks for programmers to create a PSP coding team”. It was just what I was looking for!

The nickname of the publisher was GERMAX (his blog is still online). I sent him a private message with my email and saying that I was interested. Some days later or maybe the same day, we met in Windows Live Messenger (feeling old now…). His real name was Germán, he lived in Mexico and was 4 years older than me. He immediately seemed nice to me, and I think it was reciprocal.

By the time we talked, he had already formed a team with two guys called valen_9 and Viorel. The team was named Team VGV because of their initials. They were already preparing some releases, including one called Pixelizador (by that time it was the second version). This application helped to repair dead or stuck pixels by displaying images of different colours at different speed rates. I have just seen that there are videos in YouTube that also do this process!

Small parenthesis: applications that repaired pixels were almost as common as the ones that hid files, so before knowing theirs, I had already used one called UltraScreen done by Marce82 and TheSixthHalcon (nevertheless, I think that the first version of the Pixelizador was older than UltraScreen). Then, I thought it was not too difficult for me to program it, so I did an application which was never released called NeoPixel. From what I remember, my code was horrible: the task could be easily done with loops, but instead I put an instruction for showing each colour one after another… End of the parenthesis.

In the moment I joined the team, Viorel was fired because he never collaborated, and another guy “substituted” him: The_dark. He and valen_9, who were actually called Jorge and Valentín, knew each other since they were from the same town: Badajoz (Spain). The team was renamed to Team VGT.

My relationship with them was excellent during the three years we worked together. We even started a pair of forums called TecPro and TecMax. Both were initially free hosted in 4sql.net, and they used the Icy Phoenix system. The content we published was, as their name state, related to technology (although most of it was focused on games). Everything was fully written in Spanish.

TecMax was created to substitute TecPro (I don’t remember why), and lasted for some months. We got more than 1000 users there, and then we though that we wanted to leave the free hosting to have something more professional: we founded TecMax.net.

Some time before launching TecMax, the team and me joined another website called PSPStation.org. This site was created by a group of people from TeknoPSP that was not satisfied with the way the page was directed. My colleague Gladiator told me to sign in and I did (I think I was the seventh registered user or so).

The environment at PSPStation was incredibly friendly and I learnt a lot of new things. Besides, I met new people with whom I talked many times through Messenger. Some of the nicknames I remember now are Adrin, Raisen, Zheive, Zaqui, Noninane, Aw, … Most of the times I designed signatures for them or interfaces for their homebrews. Actually, after the fiasco of EstrenosPSP, I started organizing some of the design challenges there. I even recorded some videos for their YouTube channel:

  • Design tutorials (1, 2, 3).
  • Gameplays (1).
  • PSP tutorials (1, 2, 3).
  • Showing homebrew (1, 2, 3, 4).

Probably, I haven’t been happier than in PSPStation and TecMax. Having said this… I wouldn’t participate in the creation of homebrew with the Team VGT until after taking part in my first PSP programming contest: the Scenery Beta 2008.

Chapter 8: The Scenery Beta 2008

In 2008, one of the main references in the Spanish scene, SceneBeta, organizes an international programming contest: the Scenery Beta International Homebrew Showcase (article, rules).

It consisted of different categories, one for each different platform: PSP, NintendoDS, PC and Wii. For each category, there were two subcategories: games and apps. The five most voted creations for each category received a price (from a trophy and money for the winner, to a pen drive for the last one). The votes were a combination between the users and participants votes.

From the very beginning, I was determined to participate even with an application that hid files… which was what finally happened. Ocultador 2.0 had not a graphical interface, so I decided that the main novelty would be to add one. I knew I wasn’t going to succeed since it was still not original, but my technical capabilities were poor. In hindsight, I missed lots of the fundamentals of C (and, in general, of programming) that were required to do something with some aspirations.

I programmed the application with the help of my new friend Germán. The design was done by Ozzblf, who I met in the design subforum of ESPALPSP; I admired him a lot because his designs always looked awesome to me. However, I believe that I pushed him so hard to have the interface designed quickly, that he desisted to do his best (concretely, in the screen where the folders to hide appear, the other ones are astonishing). It was completely my fault because: (1) there was enough time to substitute the prototype images for the good ones (1 minute), (2) he was doing it without expecting anything in return. This is something I’ve tried to correct in the last years: don’t put unnecessary pressure on a person that you know he/she is doing everything he/she can.

By the way, the final application was called NeoHider v1.0. I used the word “hider” as a translation of “ocultador”, but it seems it isn’t officially used anywhere. I remember having checked if it was correct, but I suppose Google set up a trap for me.

On the other hand, the Team VGT published the fourth version of the Pixelizador, which had better graphics and usability respect to previous versions. Although there were some applications of the same kind, their proposal was one of the first, so they were recognized by its originality. I recorded a video for them showing how the application worked, and it’s still in YouTube.

Many people at SceneBeta criticised me for presenting NeoHider to the contest. Their argument was that it wasn’t original enough. I knew that, but I tried to do my best and, as I’ve previously said, my capabilities were not incredible. In contrast, I received a lot of support from people of PSPStation to whom I’ll always be grateful. Without them, the Team VGT and Ozzblf the application couldn’t have been made.

Finally, I qualified 7th out of 9 participants by user votes of my category, PSP apps (user votes, final results). My colleagues in Team VGT did well enough to finish 5th (they won a customized pen drive). Another friend, Adrin, finished 3rd with an app called Project0; it was an app that included multiple tools to manage the PSP, including one to hide folders of the PSP.

Chapter 9: The Team VGT

After the Scenery Beta 2008, I started programming with the Team VGT. We weren’t really productive in terms of released software, but we made some interesting tools.

One of them was MaxFlasher v1.0. It was a software that included the following functionalities: backup of the four different flashes of the PSP, free useless space of the console, and hide/show folders. Besides, it was the first application we did that allowed choosing between English and Spanish.

The interface of MaxFlasher was done using the VLF library, which allowed to do applications with the same appearance than PSP official apps. At the time we published it, using VLF was a huge trend.

In 2009, SceneBeta was organizing again the Scenery Beta contest. We were going to participate and this time I would count as a member of the team. We planned to do another application which had multiple tools that could be used. Its final name was VGT Evolution. It was even published in QJ.net, a very prestigious site in the PSP scene. The video below shows what could be done with the application.

We were very proud of what we achieved. We felt that we could finish in the top-5, but we didn’t (results). From my point of view, there was a controversy that affected us very negatively. The rules clearly stated that the software which used some element under copyright wouldn’t be accepted. Nevertheless, there was one application which took many elements (icons, wallpapers, sounds) of Windows Vista; in fact, its name was Cristal Vista.

When designing the interface of VGT Evolution, we always chose icons with free licenses. Then, Cristal Vista appears and it’s accepted. So we agreed that it was not fair that it could compete against us and other applications which proposed designs that made sure to avoid copyright problems.

The main problem was that one of us decided to complain in public. A better idea, from my point of view, would have been to tell to the organizers privately. I suppose we do it privately as well, but the public complain was totally out of place.

The organizers understood that the app shouldn’t have been accepted, so they disqualified the author. The author of Cristal Vista had the opportunity to change the interface and apply again. He did and presented the same application with a different design: Cristal Sky. I couldn’t agree more with the organizers that it was the right way to act.

When the new of disqualification was published (source), the author announced that he may quit the scene. He received a lot of support from everybody, while we were insulted many times. If that public message hadn’t been published, we wouldn’t have received that bad press. I consider that we lost many votes because of this.

VGT Evolution finished the 11th out of 17 apps (results). We didn’t expect that results when we presented the app to the contest, so it was a bit discouraging. From that moment, I started to lose contact with my friends in Team VGT: VGT Evolution would be the last app we released.

Chapter 10: The End

Since the end of the Scenery Beta 2009, I started to leave the PSP and, in general, everything related to the PSP.

One of the causes was that I started to require more time for studying, but I also lost motivation. The same happened to my colleagues in Team VGT. For example, we stopped participating in our own website, TecMax. Since we didn’t want to close it, we substituted the forum and set up a blog instead.

We thought that the blog wouldn’t require so much time, but we were wrong. Thus, we eventually stopped posting content by the end of 2010 and we closed the website.

Something similar occurred with my participation in PSPStation, where I was in charge of organizing the design contests. Some weeks I forgot to open the contest, so I decided that more active people should do it.

Finally, I tried to do one last homebrew for the Scenery Beta 2010. This homebrew was named GuitarTab. At that time, I started to try playing the electric guitar, so I thought that it would be useful for other people and me, to have an app that could show tabs in a light device like the PSP.

The app didn’t enter the contest because it had important bugs loading the tabs. Besides, it wasn’t easily maintainable. The reason was that the tabs were images, so creating a tab consisted in creating many images. I created some and I have to recognize it was a complete nightmare… Maybe it would have been easier to load text files, and render them in some appropriate way.

Well, this is the end of my memories of the PSP scene. I hope you’ve enjoyed reading it!
